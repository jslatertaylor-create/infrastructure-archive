Curatorial Statement

Surveillance is not a technology applied to bodies. It is a technology produced by the cultural logics that decided which bodies needed watching in the first place. These four texts prove it across four centuries, four populations, and four distinct forms of watching that share a single architecture.

What Gets Watched — Simone Browne, Dark Matters

Browne's central argument is that you cannot build an honest theory of surveillance without centering Blackness — not as a special case or add-on, but as the foundation the whole system was built on. The technologies of transatlantic slavery — branding, slave passes, patrols, ship manifests, lantern laws — were not precursors to modern surveillance. They were modern surveillance. Contemporary systems like biometrics, facial recognition, and airport security screening inherit that racial logic directly, even when they present themselves as neutral.

Browne introduces two concepts that carry the book's theoretical weight. Racializing surveillance names what happens when surveillance practices draw and reinforce racial boundaries — actively producing racial categories rather than simply observing them. Dark sousveillance names the tactics, epistemologies, and freedom practices that enslaved and targeted Black people developed to resist: forged passes, coded songs, misdirection. The archive of slavery is not historical background. It is the actual theoretical infrastructure of surveillance as we know it.

"Black people are forced to straddle the line between visibility and invisibility, and neither side of that line is actually safe. The same body that gets hypervisible, invasive treatment at a TSA checkpoint is simultaneously invisible to facial recognition software trained on prototypically white faces. Hypervisibility as threat and invisibility as neglect are not opposites — they are two simultaneous operations of the same logic."

The lantern laws make this vivid: enslaved people were legally compelled to carry candles in the streets after dark so the white gaze could always locate them. Visibility was mandated, on the state's terms. That logic did not end. It was digitized. The contemporary moment — in which a body simultaneously triggers TSA hyperscrutiny and returns no results in a facial recognition search — is not a contradiction. It is the same apparatus operating on the same premises.

Simone Browne · CUNY Digital Praxis Seminar · December 2013

What Gets Hidden — Alexander Monea, The Digital Closet

Monea's central distinction is one that matters significantly: the internet did not merely reflect a homophobic culture — it actively became one. Heteronormativity has been encoded as technical infrastructure, embedded into the training data that teaches algorithms what "sexual" means, the keyword taxonomies that determine what gets surfaced, the cultural assumptions of Silicon Valley coders, and the content moderation playbooks handed to offshore workers given seconds to adjudicate community standards.

The result is what Monea calls the "digital closet" — a term he frames not as metaphor but as mechanism. To participate in digital public life increasingly requires LGBTQ+ individuals to bracket their sexual identities, not because any single policy mandates it, but because the cumulative logic of the system makes visibility costly in ways that are precise, swift, and largely undetectable to those it does not target.

"The internet did not stumble into straightness. It was built that way, incrementally, by people who could not see what they were encoding because it looked to them like nothing at all. The defaults that harm are the ones that feel like neutrality to those who set them."

The mechanism operates through a double movement of visibility: LGBTQ+ identities are rendered hypervisible as sexual threat — tripping filters trained on heteronormative assumptions — while being simultaneously erased as legitimate participants in digital public life through deprioritization, demonetization, and deplatforming. FOSTA-SESTA in 2017, framed as protecting trafficking victims, accelerated platform censorship in ways that landed hardest on LGBTQ+ communities while leaving major heteroporn producers largely intact.

Visible as Resource, Invisible as Person — Felicity Amaya Schaeffer, Unsettled Borders

Remote Video Surveillance System · US–Mexico Borderlands

The Nativision Pipeline

The tower stands on Tohono O'odham Nation territory. The sensors it uses to track movement draw on pattern-recognition techniques extracted from Apache scouts by the US military. The knowledge was abstracted, automated, and redeployed to survey the land it came from. The garden was burned. What remains is the shadow of what was taken.

Schaeffer's central argument is that the militarized surveillance apparatus at the US-Mexico border cannot be understood without centering its settler colonial foundations. The border was never a neutral line. It is a technology of occupation. Settler surveillance names the state's automated seeing that empties land of Native presence even as it scans for Native threat. Nativision is the visual and sensory knowledge of the land that Apache scouts possessed — knowledge the US military extracted, abstracted, and converted into its own arsenal, erasing the source in the process.

"You are visible as a resource and invisible as a person — which is somehow worse than either condition alone. The state sees your knowledge but not your humanity. It sees the what but not the why. And that selective seeing is what makes extraction possible."

The counter-logic Schaeffer offers is sacredscience — an Indigenous relational knowledge system that understands land not as property to be mapped but as a living ancestor to be guided by. You cannot extract from sacredscience without destroying it, because the knowledge only works inside the web of relations it emerged from. To abstract it is to kill it. What the military gained from Nativision was a shadow of what it took.

The Archive as Verdict — Jules Gill-Peterson, Histories of the Transgender Child

If Browne shows surveillance as racial infrastructure, Schaeffer as colonial extraction, and Monea as encoded heteronormativity, Gill-Peterson shows surveillance as medical governance — the use of clinical observation to produce, contain, and adjudicate gender nonconformity in children throughout the 20th century.

Gill-Peterson's archival recovery demonstrates that medicine did not discover the trans child. It produced one — through the accumulation of case files, diagnostic categories, and institutional protocols designed to identify and correct gender variance before it could become identity. The medical record was never a neutral document. It was a technology of assessment and a vehicle for enforcing gender normativity on bodies too young to contest being categorized.

The archive watched. The archive judged. The bodies catalogued knew that long before the scholars arrived to read the files.

"The bodies most surveilled were also the people whose accounts of that surveillance were systematically dismissed as not counting as evidence. The same logic governs who gets watched and who gets believed."

Curatorial Statement

Default is a design choice. When the default body encoded into a system is male, every other body becomes a deviation — not a user, but an edge case. This wing holds the evidence.

The Gender Data Gap — Caroline Criado-Perez

Criado-Perez's Invisible Women is exhaustive in the best possible way. The data gap — the systemic absence of women's data from scientific studies, urban planning, medical research, and product design — means the material world is built around a body that is presumed to be male.

The Gender of Things — María Rentetzi

Rentetzi's work adds a material history dimension: technologies have always been gendered — not just in who uses them but in how they were developed, who was credited, and what kinds of knowledge they were designed to produce. The laboratory itself is a gendered space, and the instruments inside it carry the gender politics of their manufacture. The thing and its politics arrive together.

"Instruments are not neutral mediators of nature. They are made objects — and like all made objects, they carry the politics of their making."

— Reading note on Rentetzi, The Gender of Things

The Informal Architecture of Exclusion — Coleen Carrigan

Carrigan maps the informal social infrastructure of tech and engineering workplaces — the unwritten norms that make women feel like permanent guests in rooms they helped build. The bro code is not written down. It lives in the inside joke, the after-hours Slack channel, the conference hallway. I worked in those rooms for nine years. The code was legible before I had a name for it.

Wing 003

The View from Somewhere

NashHarawayCipolla et al.Boellstorff

Curatorial Statement

Whose knowledge counts as knowledge? This wing holds the epistemological argument that makes this museum possible — the insistence that all knowledge is situated, partial, and produced from somewhere specific.

After Intersectionality — Jennifer Nash

Nash doesn't abandon intersectionality but asks what has happened to it as it moved from Black feminist critique to institutional diversity framework. The institutionalization of a concept can be a form of erasure — it gets adopted, cited, credited to everyone and no one, and the Black women who built it get acknowledged in a footnote while the framework is deployed in diversity training and HR policy.

"Intersectionality has become a traveling theory — and in the traveling, something of its political sharpness has been smoothed away."

— Reading note on Nash, Black Feminism Reimagined

The View from Nowhere Is a God Trick — Donna Haraway

Haraway's Situated Knowledges is the epistemological foundation for everything in this museum. Every exhibit in every wing is a view from somewhere. The institution that produced these readings is somewhere. The curator who assembled them is somewhere. That is not a problem to be solved — it is a condition to be named and accountable to.

The Cyborg Manifesto extends this argument into political form: if the fantasy of a unified, essential self is a fiction that serves existing power arrangements, then embracing the cyborg — the hybrid, the partial, the constructed — is an act of resistance. The boundaries are not natural. They are made. They can be remade.

"The only way to find a larger vision is to be somewhere in particular."

— Donna Haraway, Situated Knowledges

Polyphonic Knowledge — Cipolla, Gupta, Rubin, Willey

The introduction to Queer Feminist Science Studies names what this museum tries to do: produce knowledge that is simultaneously rigorous and situated, analytical and embodied. The introduction is itself a methodology — multiple voices, multiple disciplinary locations, refusing the false comfort of a single authoritative perspective. The polyphony is not a limitation. It is the argument.

Making and Knowing — Tom Boellstorff

Boellstorff recovers the Greek concept of techne to argue that technology is always already an epistemological claim — that how we produce things is also how we produce truth. This museum is a techne argument. The digital museum format is not a container for the argument. It is the argument.

Wing 004

Other Arrangements, Still Possible

DokumaciBenjaminClarke & HarawayBahng

Curatorial Statement

Infrastructure can also be unmade. This wing holds the tools of refusal — activist affordances, abolitionist imagination, and speculative possibility as counter-infrastructure.

Activist Affordances — Arseli Dokumaci

Dokumaci begins from disability studies and arrives somewhere surprising: the idea that marginalized people routinely create affordances from environments never designed to support them. This is not resilience as compliment. It is a critique of the environment. If disabled people must constantly improvise to survive in spaces built without them, the problem is the space. But Dokumaci also insists on the creativity and dignity of that improvisation — activist affordances are a form of knowledge production that the designers of those environments cannot access from their position of ease.

"The refusal to be erased by a system is itself a form of knowledge. It is expertise the system cannot see from inside itself."

— Reading note on Dokumaci, Activist Affordances

Abolitionist Tools — Ruha Benjamin

Benjamin's Race After Technology provides the political frame. The New Jim Code — her term for racial bias encoded into technical systems — is not an accident or a bug. It is a feature of systems built within and for a racially stratified society. The solution is not more diverse hiring or better bias audits. The solution is abolition: the refusal to accept the premise that these systems, as designed, are salvageable. Abolitionist tools ask what it would look like to build from the ground of justice rather than retrofitting justice onto systems built for something else entirely.

Ruha Benjamin: Race After Technology

Making Kin, Not Population — Clarke & Haraway

Clarke and Haraway assemble arguments for reimagining reproduction, population, and care outside the logics of capitalism and colonialism. Making kin is a counter-infrastructure practice — it refuses the normative family form as the only model for human connection and builds alternative structures of obligation and care. The infrastructure of belonging can be redesigned. It has been before.

"Low Fences" — Aimee Bahng

Low fences do not keep the speculative out. They mark the edge of what we agreed to call the yard — and nothing more. Beyond them: other arrangements, still possible.

— In response to Bahng's "Low Fences," 2026

Bahng's "Low Fences" offers a speculative geography — the recognition that the boundaries we treat as natural or permanent are agreements, not facts. The speculative is not outside the real. It is the real, waiting for permission to exist.

Curator on Record

Jeo Slater-Taylor

PhD Candidate · Science & Technology Studies · Virginia Tech · 2026

I spent nine years building infrastructure. Federal systems, integration architectures, data flows across agencies. I held a TS/SCI clearance and sat in rooms where technical decisions were made that would affect thousands of people — and those decisions were described exclusively in technical terms.

Nobody called them cultural decisions. Nobody called them political decisions. They were requirements, they were constraints, they were specifications. The language of technical neutrality was so pervasive that I used it myself, fluently, for nearly a decade.

This museum is my first year of unlearning that fluency — or rather, of learning to see it as a fluency with a politics. The texts in these wings gave me something I didn't have as a practicing Solutions Architect: a vocabulary for what was happening in those rooms that the rooms themselves refused to supply.

Browne taught me that the systems I built had histories I wasn't trained to see. Criado-Perez taught me that the bodies I implicitly encoded into my designs were not generic. Haraway taught me that the position I spoke from was not universal. Dokumaci and Benjamin taught me that the people most harmed by those systems are also the ones most equipped to critique and dismantle them.

My dissertation argues that culture functions as sociotechnical infrastructure — that cultural practices shape who gets recognized as an expert, whose knowledge counts, and how technical systems get built and legitimized. That argument is not separate from this semester's reading. It is this semester's reading, made legible by nine years of living inside the infrastructure I'm now studying.

This museum is a beginning. It is also, I think, the most honest thing I've made all year.
